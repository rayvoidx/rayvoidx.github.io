Frequently asked questions

How many calories do I burn a day? What is TDEE?

Total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) is every calorie your body uses in a day — the energy burned at rest (BMR) plus digestion, daily movement, and exercise. It's the number to eat around if you want your weight to stay the same. This TDEE calculator multiplies your basal metabolic rate by an activity multiplier for how active you are, so it captures your real day, not just what your body needs lying still. Most adults burn between 1,600 and 3,200 kcal a day depending on body size, sex, and activity level.

How does this TDEE calculator work — formula and worked example?

It first estimates your basal metabolic rate with the Mifflin-St Jeor equation: 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age, +5 for men or −161 for women. That BMR is then multiplied by an activity factor from 1.2 (sedentary) to 1.9 (extra active) to get your TDEE. Worked example: a 30-year-old man, 80 kg, 178 cm has a BMR of about 1,770 kcal; at moderately active (×1.55) his TDEE is roughly 2,740 kcal a day. Metric and imperial entries (ft/in, lb) are converted internally, so the result is identical either way.

Which activity level should I pick, and what happens if I overestimate it?

Sedentary means a desk job with little or no exercise; lightly active is 1-3 light workouts a week; moderately active is 3-5 workouts a week; very active is hard exercise 6-7 days a week; extra active covers physical jobs or twice-daily training. Most people overestimate their activity level and end up eating more than they actually burn — if your weight isn't moving toward your goal after 2-3 weeks, try the tier below the one you picked. Choosing sedentary when you're really lightly active can overstate your deficit by 150-250 kcal a day, enough to stall a cut.

Mild cut, standard cut or bulk — how many calories should I eat to lose or gain weight?

This calculator shows a mild cut (10% below TDEE, roughly 0.25-0.3 kg a week) and a standard cut (20% below TDEE, roughly 0.5-0.6 kg a week) for weight loss, plus a bulk (15% above TDEE) for building muscle with a lean surplus. The weekly rate uses the common estimate that about 7,700 kcal equals 1 kg of body fat; individual results vary with water weight, sleep, and training. A mild cut is easier to sustain and preserves more muscle; a standard cut is faster but harder to stick to for long periods, so pick based on the deficit you can maintain consistently.

Is my age, height and weight sent to a server?

No. Every calculation in this TDEE calculator runs entirely in your browser; your age, sex, height, weight and activity level never leave your device. They are only saved to your browser's localStorage so the form is pre-filled on your next visit, and you can clear them anytime from your browser settings.
